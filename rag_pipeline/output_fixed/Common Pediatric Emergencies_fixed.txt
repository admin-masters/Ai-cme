COMMON PEDIATRIC EMERGENCIES;PHYSIOLOGY FIRST TRIAGE AND CORE EMERGENCY SUPPORTS;ABCDE TRIAGE AND INVESTIGATION DECISIONS IN THE FIRST HOUR;
In busy Indian outpatient and emergency settings, prioritize physiology over diagnosis. Use an 
##ABCDE## screen with parallel actions: open the airway (position, suction, jaw thrust), give oxygen 
if the child is cyanosed or has low ##SpO2##, and start ##BVM## ventilation early if breathing is 
inadequate. For circulation, treat shock before hypotension: cold extremities, weak pulse, prolonged 
capillary refill, altered sensorium, and oliguria warrant rapid vascular access and reassessment after 
small fluid boluses. Check bedside glucose early in any child with seizures, altered behavior, or severe 
malnutrition, and treat hypoglycemia immediately.  

Investigations should follow stabilization and answer a decision. Do not delay antibiotics, dextrose, 
bronchodilators, or anticonvulsants for laboratory confirmation. In resource-limited facilities, a 
focused set is usually sufficient: bedside glucose, hemoglobin, lactate if available, and targeted tests 
such as dengue ##NS1## in the first 5 days of fever or malaria testing in endemic areas. Escalate and 
refer early when the child needs respiratory support beyond low-flow oxygen, repeated fluid boluses 
or vasoactive support, persistent altered mental status, or has red flags such as severe respiratory 
distress, shock, or seizures without recovery. Communicate a pre-referral bundle (airway secured, 
oxygen on, ##IV## or ##IO## access, first doses given, documentation of vitals and weight estimate) 
to reduce deterioration during transport.

COMMON PEDIATRIC EMERGENCIES;PHYSIOLOGY FIRST TRIAGE AND CORE EMERGENCY SUPPORTS;OXYGEN DEVICE SELECTION AND SATURATION TARGETS WITH SPO2 ARTIFACT TROUBLESHOOTING;
Oxygen is a time-critical drug in many pediatric emergencies, but both under-treatment and 
hyperoxia can be harmful. For most acutely ill children without cyanotic congenital heart disease, aim 
for ##SpO2## 94–98% and treat sustained ##SpO2## <90% with supplemental oxygen while 
addressing the cause. Start with low-flow nasal cannula (about 1–2 L/min in infants and 2–4 L/min in 
older children), escalate to simple face mask, and use a non-rebreather mask for severe hypoxemia 
while preparing definitive respiratory support. Escalation to ##HFNC## or ##CPAP## is appropriate 
when work of breathing remains high or oxygen need is rising despite low-flow oxygen, but these 
require close monitoring for fatigue and air leak.  

Pulse oximetry can be misleading. Poor perfusion (shock, hypothermia), motion artifact, ambient 
light, nail polish, and skin pigmentation can produce falsely reassuring readings; overestimation of 
true arterial saturation has been reported in darker skin tones, especially at lower saturations. When 
the ##SpO2## does not match clinical status, troubleshoot (warm the limb, change probe site, 
ensure good waveform) and prioritize clinical signs such as respiratory effort, mental status, and 
perfusion. In low-resource settings, trend simple parameters (##RR##, ##HR##, capillary refill, urine 
output, sensorium) and document response to oxygen and bronchodilators. During transport, secure 
the oxygen source, monitor for hypoventilation after sedatives, prevent hypothermia, and 
communicate target saturations and escalation triggers to the receiving facility.

stability and easy flush, and secure well because dislodgement is common during transfer. Aspiration 
of marrow may be absent and is not required if flush is easy and there is no soft-tissue swelling.

COMMON PEDIATRIC EMERGENCIES;RESUSCITATION AND ARRHYTHMIA EMERGENCIES;PEDIATRIC CARDIAC ARREST CPR DEFIBRILLATION AND MEDICATION SEQUENCE;
In pediatric arrest, outcomes depend most on early recognition, uninterrupted high-quality ##CPR##, 
and  timely  defibrillation  for  shockable  rhythms.  Start  compressions  immediately on  a firm  surface, 
aiming for a depth of about one-third the anterior–posterior chest diameter, full recoil, and minimal 
pauses.  With  two  rescuers,  use  a  15:2  compression-to-ventilation  ratio;  if  an  advanced  airway  is 
placed,  ventilate  about  1  breath  every  2–3  seconds  without  stopping  compressions.  Attach 
monitor/defibrillator as soon as available and obtain ##IV## or ##IO## access early.  

For shockable rhythms (##VF## or pulseless ##VT##), deliver a shock at 2 J/kg, resume ##CPR## 
immediately for 2 minutes, then reassess rhythm. Escalate energy to 4 J/kg for the next shock; 
subsequent shocks can be ≥4 J/kg (not exceeding 10 J/kg or adult maximum). Give epinephrine 0.01 
mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 0.1 mg/mL) ##IV##/##IO## every 3–5 minutes, starting after the second shock, 
and consider amiodarone 5 mg/kg or lidocaine for refractory shockable arrest. For nonshockable 

 
rhythms (asystole or ##PEA##), focus on effective ##CPR##, epinephrine every 3–5 minutes, and 
rapidly correcting reversible causes.  

Actively search for and treat reversible causes (the Hs and Ts): hypoxia, hypovolemia, hydrogen ion 
(acidosis), hypo/hyperkalemia and hypoglycemia, hypothermia, tension pneumothorax, tamponade, 
toxins, and thrombosis. In India, hypoxia from pneumonia, asthma, bronchiolitis, drowning, and 
sepsis-related shock are common precipitants; correct airway and ventilation problems early and 
reassess response every 2 minutes.

After sustained ##ROSC##, treat the child as critically unstable even if the blood pressure is normal. 
Prioritize oxygenation and ventilation: target ##SpO2## 94–98% (or the child’s usual saturation in 
cyanotic heart disease) and avoid both hypocapnia and severe hypercapnia. Use continuous 
##ETCO2## if available, and reassess tube position and chest rise after any movement. Maintain 
ageappropriate perfusion with careful fluids and vasoactive support as needed, and avoid 
hypotension, which is associated with worse neurological outcomes.  

Temperature control is a core intervention. Prevent fever and actively treat hyperthermia; manage 
shivering and discomfort with appropriate analgesia and sedation. Check glucose and correct 
hypoglycemia; avoid wide swings in glucose during the first day. Monitor for seizures (clinical or 
electrographic if available) and treat promptly. Obtain targeted investigations to identify the cause 
and complications (##ECG##, bedside echo if available, chest radiograph, electrolytes, blood gas, 
lactate), but do not destabilize the patient for non-urgent tests.  

For India-specific transfer, use a transport-ready checklist: secure airway and oxygen source, confirm 
ventilator settings or bagging plan, ensure at least one reliable vascular access (preferably two), start 
maintenance fluids with clear rates, prepare vasoactive infusions if shock recurs, document 
neurostatus and pupillary findings, and send written medication times and doses. Early 
communication with the receiving ##PICU## improves continuity and reduces avoidable delays.

COMMON PEDIATRIC EMERGENCIES;RESUSCITATION AND ARRHYTHMIA EMERGENCIES;BRADYCARDIA AND TACHYARRHYTHMIA WITH A PULSE EMERGENCY DECISIONS;
Treat pediatric bradycardia and tachyarrhythmia as airway and perfusion problems first. Start with 
##ABCDE## support, oxygen, and a monitor, and correct hypoxia, hypovolemia, fever, and toxins. In 
bradycardia, the key decision point is cardiopulmonary compromise. If ##HR## is <60/min with poor 
perfusion despite effective oxygenation and ventilation, start ##CPR##, obtain ##IV##/##IO## access, 
and give epinephrine 0.01 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 0.1 mg/mL) every 3–5 minutes. Consider atropine 
0.02 mg/kg (minimum 0.1 mg; maximum 0.5 mg per dose in a child) when increased vagal tone or 
primary ##AV## block is likely, and seek expert help early for pacing.  

For tachyarrhythmia with a pulse, determine whether the child is unstable (hypotension, altered 
mental status, signs of shock, or acute heart failure). Unstable tachyarrhythmia requires synchronized 
cardioversion: 0.5–1 J/kg, increasing to 2 J/kg if needed. In stable, regular narrow-complex 
tachycardia consistent with ##SVT##, attempt vagal maneuvers, then give adenosine 0.1 mg/kg rapid 
##IV## (maximum 6 mg) followed by a flush; if ineffective, give 0.2 mg/kg (maximum 12 mg). 
Widecomplex tachycardia should be treated as ventricular tachycardia until proven otherwise; avoid 
verapamil in infants and consult urgently.  

In India, delays in electrical cardioversion occur when sedation, defibrillator pads, or expertise are 
limited. If cardioversion is needed, prioritize timely shock with the safest available sedation strategy 
rather than prolonged attempts at pharmacologic conversion in an unstable child.

Stridor is a sign of upper airway narrowing; rapid deterioration can occur with agitation. Keep the 
child with the caregiver, avoid painful procedures, and give humidified oxygen by blow-by if 
hypoxemic. Use the tempo and associated features to triage. Viral croup usually has a barky cough 
and hoarse voice with low-grade fever; sudden onset choking, unilateral wheeze, or voice change 
raises concern for an inhaled foreign body. Urticaria, facial swelling, or hypotension suggests 
anaphylaxis; drooling, muffled voice, tripod posture, and toxic appearance suggest epiglottitis or 
deep neck infection. Do not attempt oropharyngeal examination with a tongue depressor if 
epiglottitis is suspected; prioritize controlled airway management with senior help.  

For typical croup, a single dose of dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg orally (maximum 10–12 mg) reduces 
symptoms and revisits. Nebulized adrenaline is indicated for moderate to severe croup with stridor at 
rest or marked retractions; a commonly used dose is 0.5 mL/kg of 1:1000 adrenaline (maximum 5 
mL) via nebulizer. Adrenaline has a rapid effect but may wear off, so observe for at least 2–4 hours 
after the last dose and reassess work of breathing and ##SpO2##.  

Discharge is reasonable when the child has no stridor at rest, maintains hydration, and has stable 
##SpO2## on room air. Admit or refer urgently for persistent stridor at rest after therapy, exhaustion, 
poor air entry, cyanosis, recurrent need for nebulized adrenaline, suspicion of bacterial 
tracheitis/epiglottitis, or when safe monitoring at home is unlikely.

COMMON PEDIATRIC EMERGENCIES;ACUTE AIRWAY AND RESPIRATORY EMERGENCIES;BRONCHIOLITIS SUPPORTIVE CARE OXYGEN THRESHOLDS AND RESPIRATORY SUPPORT ESCALATION;
Bronchiolitis is a clinical diagnosis in infants, commonly due to ##RSV##, and management is 
supportive. Minimize handling, position the infant, and use gentle nasal suction to improve feeding 
and reduce work of breathing. Routine antibiotics, bronchodilators, and steroids do not improve 
outcomes in typical bronchiolitis and should be avoided unless another diagnosis is suspected.  

Oxygen decisions should be consistent and documented. Admit or observe infants with apnea 
(reported or witnessed), exhaustion, inability to feed, or persistent ##SpO2## below accepted 
thresholds. Many guidelines use <90% on room air for children ≥6 weeks, and <92% for babies <6 
weeks or any age with comorbidity. Start with low-flow nasal oxygen and reassess clinical work of 
breathing rather than chasing a number. Escalate respiratory support when fatigue, rising oxygen 
requirement, or persistent severe retractions occur despite low-flow oxygen. ##HFNC## can reduce 
work of breathing in selected infants, but requires monitoring for deterioration; ##CPAP## is 
preferred when there is significant hypercapnia, apnea, or failure of ##HFNC##, and should trigger 
early referral to a higher-level unit.  

High-risk groups include premature infants, those with congenital heart disease, chronic lung disease, 
or very young age. In India, safe discharge requires a reliable caregiver, feeding adequacy, and access 
to follow-up, because deterioration may occur overnight. Provide clear return precautions for apnea, 
poor feeding, increasing retractions, or lethargy. References:

Add ipratropium bromide in moderate to severe exacerbations and give systemic steroids early (for 
example prednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day, maximum 40–50 mg, or equivalent) because onset is delayed. 
If response is inadequate after initial inhaled therapy, give ##IV## magnesium sulfate 40–50 mg/kg

Escalate early to a higher-level unit when there is persistent severe respiratory distress after firsthour 
therapy, exhaustion, altered mental status, rising or normalizing ##PaCO2## in a tiring child, 
recurrent need for continuous bronchodilator therapy, or impending respiratory failure (bradycardia, 
poor air entry, or persistent ##SpO2## <92% despite high oxygen). If intubation is required, it should 
be performed by the most experienced clinician available; prepare for hypotension, use 
lungprotective ventilation with permissive hypercapnia when appropriate, and continue 
bronchodilators and steroids. In India, arrange early transfer rather than waiting for complete failure 
in facilities without continuous monitoring.

Discharge planning begins early because relapse is common when caregivers cannot continue 
treatment correctly at home. Discharge is usually safe when the child has minimal work of breathing, 
speaks/feeds comfortably, has stable ##SpO2## on room air, and requires short-acting beta-agonist 
no more often than every 4 hours. Before leaving, demonstrate and observe inhaler technique with a 
spacer, confirm caregiver understanding of doses and frequency, and document weight-based steroid 
plan if prescribed.  
Provide a written asthma action plan that separates daily controller therapy from reliever use. Ensure 
access to and adherence with an inhaled corticosteroid (##ICS##) for persistent asthma, and consider 
step-up after a severe exacerbation. For preschool wheeze, clarify whether episodes are 
viraltriggered or multi-trigger and align controller choices with national guidance; avoid labeling 
every wheezing episode as infection. Address modifiable triggers common in India such as tobacco

Pneumothorax is a time-critical reversible cause of sudden deterioration and should be considered 
when respiratory distress worsens abruptly, especially with unilateral reduced air entry, 
hyperresonance, increasing hypoxemia, or evolving shock. Suspect tension pneumothorax when 
these findings are accompanied by severe distress and obstructive shock physiology (tachycardia, 
poor perfusion, narrow pulse pressure, or sudden bradycardia). 

Do not wait for radiography in an unstable child. If tension pneumothorax is clinically likely, 
decompress immediately, then proceed to definitive tube thoracostomy when feasible because 
recurrence after needle decompression alone is common. Document the immediate response 
(improved ##SpO2##, reduced distress, improved perfusion) to guide ongoing management. In a 
stable child, confirm with chest radiograph or bedside ultrasound when available, and manage based 
on size, symptoms, and underlying lung disease. 

In mechanically ventilated children, pneumothorax can worsen quickly. Rising airway pressures, 
sudden desaturation, or unexpected bradycardia should trigger an immediate air-leak assessment 

and readiness to decompress. Pneumothorax should also be considered in a newborn who initially 
improves with ventilation and then deteriorates during resuscitation. 

Transfer early when a chest drain is needed, oxygen requirement escalates, underlying pathology 
such as necrotizing pneumonia is suspected, or any hemodynamic instability is present, because 
definitive care may require ventilation and skilled drain management.

COMMON PEDIATRIC EMERGENCIES;ANAPHYLAXIS EMERGENCY RECOGNITION AND EPINEPHRINE THERAPY;INTRAMUSCULAR EPINEPHRINE DOSING FLUID RESUSCITATION AND OBSERVATION DECISIONS;
Anaphylaxis is a clinical diagnosis; do not wait for hypotension or skin findings. Suspect it when there 
is acute onset of airway or breathing compromise (stridor, wheeze, hypoxemia), circulation 
compromise (collapse, weak pulses), or severe gastrointestinal symptoms after a likely trigger. Give 
epinephrine immediately as the first-line treatment: 0.01 mg/kg of 1 mg/mL (1:1000) ##IM## into 
the mid-anterolateral thigh (maximum 0.5 mg), and repeat every 5–15 minutes if symptoms persist.  
Place the child supine with legs elevated unless breathing is easier sitting up. Provide high-flow 
oxygen, establish ##IV## access, and give isotonic fluid boluses (for example 20 mL/kg) for shock, 
repeating with reassessment. Adjuncts (after epinephrine) include inhaled salbutamol for 
bronchospasm, antihistamines for urticaria, and corticosteroids for persistent symptoms, but none 
replace epinephrine. Escalate to an epinephrine infusion when there is refractory shock or repeated 
##IM## doses are required, and arrange urgent transfer to a facility capable of continuous 
monitoring.  

Observe all children for recurrence, because biphasic reactions can occur. A minimum observation 
period of 4 hours after the last epinephrine dose is common; admit for prolonged monitoring when 
there was severe respiratory compromise, hypotension, need for multiple epinephrine doses, 
comorbid asthma, or when access to emergency care is limited. In India, discharge counseling should 
include trigger avoidance, a written emergency plan, and access to epinephrine (auto-injector when 
available, or prefilled syringe strategies where policy allows).

Shock is a clinical diagnosis and often precedes hypotension in children. Look for tachycardia out of

A structured coma pathway reduces missed reversible causes in Indian settings where infection, 
metabolic derangement, and poisoning overlap. Start with ##ABCDE## support plus bedside glucose, 
and treat while investigating rather than waiting for a definitive label. 

In the first minutes, treat what kills fastest. If glucose is low or not immediately known, give dextrose

repeated sedating boluses. If seizures are present or suspected (subtle eye deviation, unexplained

therapy is required.

unstable child. A cholinergic or opioid toxidrome suggests poisoning; prioritize airway toileting and 
ventilation, and give antidote when clinical criteria are met. Severe malaria should be considered in 
endemic areas when coma, recurrent seizures, anemia, jaundice, or hypoglycemia coexist; arrange 
urgent malaria testing and treat severe features without delay.

presentations are short, self-limited events, and the key decision is excluding ##CNS## infection 
while avoiding unnecessary imaging. Classify the event because this governs evaluation and 
counseling. Simple febrile seizure is generalized, lasts <15 minutes, and occurs once in 24 hours; 
complex febrile seizure includes focal features, duration >15 minutes, or recurrence within 24 hours. 

Immediate priorities are stabilization only if the seizure is ongoing or the child is not recovering. If 
convulsions persist beyond 5 minutes, treat as an acute seizure using a single benzodiazepine 
strategy (for example buccal or intranasal midazolam) and prepare for ventilation if respiratory 
depression develops. Avoid repeated benzodiazepine stacking; the predictable complication is 
hypoventilation in a child who otherwise might have recovered. 

After the seizure, reassess for meningitis or encephalitis. Red flags include persistent altered 
sensorium, neck stiffness, bulging fontanelle, petechiae or purpura, shock signs, focal neurological 
deficit, or toxic appearance out of proportion to fever. When these are present, manage as suspected 
infection: stabilize, obtain cultures if feasible, start antibiotics when indicated, and plan 
neuroimaging or ##LP## based on raised intracranial pressure risk. Routine neuroimaging and 
routine ##EEG## are not required for simple febrile seizures. 

Discharge is reasonable when the child returns to baseline, vital signs are stable, there are no 
meningeal signs or persistent neurological abnormalities, the fever source plan is clear, and 
caregivers receive written return precautions. In India, lower the threshold for observation or 

 
admission when follow-up is unreliable or travel barriers are significant.

Hypoglycemia should be assumed and treated rapidly in any child with seizures, altered sensorium,

give ##IM## glucagon when available and initiate enteral feeds as soon as the airway is protected.

the child is stable. Investigate precipitants such as sepsis, malaria, prolonged fasting, adrenal 
insufficiency, or insulin exposure. In ##SAM## or severe infection, recurrent hypoglycemia is 
common; maintain scheduled feeding, provide nighttime feeds when feasible, and avoid long periods 
without carbohydrate. Document treatment times and repeat glucose checks, because missed

Diabetic ketoacidosis (##DKA##) is defined by hyperglycemia, ketonemia/ketonuria, and metabolic 
acidosis, and is managed by controlled rehydration, insulin, and electrolyte correction. Initial 
assessment should identify shock, altered mental status, and Kussmaul breathing. Obtain baseline

replacement over 36–48 hours) and reassess clinically and with labs. Start regular insulin infusion 
(0.05–0.1 units/kg/hour) after the first hour of fluids, once potassium is known and urine output is 
established. Avoid insulin bolus.

Poisoning presentations in India often involve household chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and 
agricultural pesticides. Stabilize using ##ABCDE##, check glucose, and treat hypoxia, seizures, and 
shock immediately. History should capture the agent, dose estimate, time of exposure, co-ingestants, 
and intent (including adolescent self-harm). Examine for toxidromes: cholinergic (salivation, 
lacrimation, bronchorrhea, bradycardia), opioid (miosis, respiratory depression), sympathomimetic 
(tachycardia, hypertension, agitation), and sedative-hypnotic (coma with hypoventilation). Obtain an 
##ECG## and targeted labs when they will change management.  

Decontamination decisions should be conservative and airway-first. Activated charcoal (1 g/kg, 
maximum 50 g) can be considered within about 1 hour of a potentially toxic ingestion if the child is 
alert with a protected airway, but it is not routine and is contraindicated when aspiration risk is high. 
Gastric lavage should be rare and considered only for a life-threatening ingestion presenting very 
early, and only with an intubated, protected airway; routine lavage increases harm and should not be 
used for most ingestions. Do not use charcoal for hydrocarbons, corrosives, or heavy metals such as 
iron.  

For hydrocarbon ingestion (kerosene), the major risk is aspiration pneumonitis. Do not induce 
vomiting or perform lavage; provide supportive oxygen, observe for cough, tachypnea, or hypoxemia, 
and obtain a chest radiograph only when symptomatic. Early consultation with a poison information 
service (when available) and clear documentation of timelines and evolving symptoms improves 
safety and avoids unnecessary antidote use.

weight, concentration in the bag, infusion rate, and total mg to be delivered in each phase. Two

In India, venomous snakebite and scorpion sting require rapid supportive care and early decisions 
about antivenom and cardiopulmonary support. For suspected snakebite, immobilize the limb with a 
splint, keep the child calm, remove constricting items, and avoid tourniquets, cutting, or suction.

coagulopathy (non-clotting ##20WBCT##), spontaneous bleeding, or rapidly progressive swelling.

supportive care. Ventilatory support is life-saving for neurotoxic bites; do not delay referral when 
respiratory weakness appears.  

Scorpion envenomation causes autonomic storm with sweating, tachycardia, cold extremities, and 
may progress to pulmonary edema and cardiogenic shock. Early prazosin is a key intervention: 30 
micrograms/kg orally, repeated every 3–6 hours until extremities are warm and perfusion improves. 
Provide oxygen, treat shock cautiously, and use inotropes such as dobutamine when there is 
myocardial dysfunction. Severe cases need monitoring in a high-dependency unit or ##ICU## and 
early transfer when ventilation or inotropes are required.

Initial burn management is airway, breathing, and circulation first, with early recognition of 
inhalation injury and shock. Suspect inhalation injury when there is facial burn, singed nasal hair, 
soot in the mouth, hoarseness, stridor, wheeze, or enclosed-space exposure. Secure the airway early

possible. Estimate burn size using %##TBSA## (excluding simple erythema) to guide fluids and

fixed truth. Different formulas are used in practice (for example Parkland-style 3–4

available and document the chosen formula. Give half of the calculated resuscitation volume in the 
first 8 hours from the time of burn and the remainder over the next 16 hours, using isotonic 
crystalloid. Add dextrose-containing maintenance in young children to reduce hypoglycemia risk.

may be used in small infants) and reassess respiratory effort, lung auscultation, and weight because 
fluid overload worsens outcomes. Early transfer is indicated for large %##TBSA## burns, suspected 
inhalation injury, electrical or chemical burns, circumferential limb or chest burns, burns involving 
face, hands, feet, genitalia, or major joints, or when critical care monitoring is unavailable.

Head injury triage balances missing clinically important ##TBI## against unnecessary ##CT## 
radiation. Start with resuscitation and cervical spine precautions when indicated, then document 
##GCS##, pupillary response, and neurological exam. Urgent ##CT## and referral are indicated with 
red flags such as persistent ##GCS## <14, focal neurological deficit, signs of basilar skull fracture, 
palpable skull fracture, penetrating injury, seizure, or persistent abnormal behavior.  

For children who are otherwise stable but not clearly low risk, structured observation is often safer 
than immediate imaging. Decision tools such as ##PECARN## identify intermediate-risk features 
where observation for 4–6 hours with repeated neurochecks is appropriate: repeated vomiting, brief 
loss of consciousness, severe headache, significant scalp hematoma (especially non-frontal in 
infants), or severe mechanism. Deterioration during observation (worsening headache, increasing 
drowsiness, repeated vomiting, new deficit) should trigger imaging and escalation.  

Discharge is appropriate when the child is neurologically normal and reliable supervision is available. 
Provide written advice on warning signs that require urgent return: worsening headache, repeated 
vomiting, confusion, seizure, abnormal behavior, weakness, or persistent drowsiness. In India, 
emphasize night-time observation by caregivers, avoidance of sedating cough syrups or alcohol 
exposure in adolescents, and early return if transport barriers make delayed presentation likely.

Abdominal pain with vomiting can represent a time-critical surgical emergency. Red flags include 
bilious vomiting, progressive distension, peritonism, severe localized tenderness, shock, and inability 
to pass stool or flatus. Resuscitate with ##IV## fluids when needed, keep the child nil per os, provide 
analgesia, and obtain early surgical consultation rather than waiting for complete diagnostic 
certainty.  

Intussusception should be suspected in infants and toddlers with episodic severe pain, pallor, 
vomiting, or unexplained lethargy, even without classic “currant jelly” stool. Ultrasound is the 
preferred imaging test when available. Stabilize first and refer urgently for pneumatic or hydrostatic 
enema reduction in stable patients; peritonitis, perforation, or shock require operative management.

guarding, and anorexia; fever may be absent early. Ultrasound-first strategies reduce radiation 
exposure; ##CT## is reserved for equivocal cases when it will change management. Start antibiotics 
when appendicitis is likely and arrange surgery.

delay for imaging when suspicion is high, because salvage declines after 4–6 hours. Incarcerated

Ingested foreign body and caustic ingestion decisions should be symptom-led because early “do-not-
do” actions prevent harm. For suspected esophageal foreign body, act on symptoms first. Drooling, 
dysphagia, chest or neck pain, inability to swallow secretions, or respiratory symptoms are urgent 
endoscopy indications. Even asymptomatic esophageal objects generally require removal within 24 
hours because prolonged impaction increases mucosal injury risk. 

Use an explicit imaging strategy. Obtain a plain radiograph for radiopaque objects (coins, many 
magnets, button batteries). Do not be reassured by a normal radiograph when a radiolucent object is 
plausible and symptoms persist; endoscopy decisions are clinical. Frame endoscopy urgency for 
trainees as emergent (within about 2 hours), urgent (within 24 hours), and elective (beyond 24 
hours) based on clinical status, object type, time since ingestion, and location. Button battery in the 
esophagus and multiple magnets (or magnet plus metal) require urgent specialist management even 
if the child initially looks well. 

Caustic ingestion management begins with airway protection and strict avoidance of harmful 
interventions. Do not induce vomiting, attempt neutralization, perform routine gastric lavage, or give 
activated charcoal for corrosives. Provide supportive care, oral rinsing, analgesia, and early transfer 
when there is drooling, stridor, hoarseness, persistent vomiting, severe chest or abdominal pain, or 
any evidence of perforation. Referral notes should document time of ingestion, object type, imaging 
findings, and last oral intake to reduce delay to endoscopy.

Asphyxial emergencies require immediate ventilation-focused care. For witnessed choking with 
severe airway obstruction, use age-specific maneuvers and avoid blind finger sweeps. In infants, give 

 
 
5 back slaps followed by 5 chest thrusts, repeating until the object is expelled or the infant becomes 
unresponsive. In older children, use abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver) until effective cough or 
collapse.  

If the child becomes unresponsive, start ##CPR## immediately. After each cycle, open the mouth and 
remove a visible object; do not perform blind sweeps. Provide rescue breaths with attention to chest 
rise and call for emergency transport. After recovery, persistent cough, unilateral wheeze, voice 
change, or recurrent pneumonia suggests an inhaled foreign body. A normal chest radiograph does 
not exclude aspiration; arrange urgent referral for bronchoscopy when suspicion is high.  

Drowning is primarily hypoxic injury. Begin rescue breaths as soon as the child is removed from the 
water, then provide ##CPR## if there is no pulse. After ##ROSC## or in a breathing child, give oxygen, 
treat bronchospasm if present, correct hypothermia, and monitor for delayed respiratory failure. 
Observation is appropriate for asymptomatic children with normal exam and stable ##SpO2## after a 
period of monitoring (commonly 4–8 hours); admit when there is persistent cough, tachypnea, 
hypoxemia, abnormal lung exam, altered mental status, or need for oxygen or ventilatory support.  
Do not give prophylactic antibiotics or steroids unless there is clear evidence of infection.

Neonatal sepsis can present with subtle signs: poor feeding, temperature instability, apnea, lethargy, 
respiratory distress, or hypoglycemia. Differentiate early-onset sepsis (first 72 hours, often maternal 
risk factors) from late-onset sepsis (after 72 hours, often environmental or device-related), because 
likely pathogens and resistance differ across Indian settings.  

Obtain blood culture before antibiotics when feasible, but do not delay treatment in an unstable 
neonate. Start empiric antibiotics based on local policy and resistance patterns. A common first-line 
regimen for early-onset sepsis is ampicillin plus an aminoglycoside (gentamicin or amikacin). Avoid 
routine third-generation cephalosporins as first line because they promote resistance and fungal 
infection; reserve cefotaxime for suspected meningitis or specific indications. For late-onset sepsis, 
add coverage for staphylococci and consider broader gram-negative coverage when 
##NICU##acquired infection is likely, guided by the unit antibiogram.  

Review therapy at 48 hours. If cultures are negative and the baby is clinically well with improving 
parameters, stop antibiotics and reassess alternate diagnoses. If cultures are positive, narrow 
antibiotics to the most effective agent and define duration by syndrome (bacteremia, pneumonia, 
meningitis). In India, antimicrobial stewardship in neonatal units is essential, because resistant 
organisms are common and prolonged empiric therapy increases necrotizing enterocolitis and 
invasive candidiasis risk. Continuous monitoring for apnea, perfusion, and glucose is part of sepsis 
care.
